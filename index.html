Step 1 — Pre-fermentation
Day 0: Must preparation
Sanitize everything
Day 0, before anything else
Use potassium metabisulfite solution (1 tsp per gallon of water) to sanitize fermenter, airlock, spoon, hydrometer. Let air dry — no rinsing needed.
Prepare the must
Day 0
Add juice concentrate to fermenter, top with water to 1 gallon. Stir thoroughly. Measure OG with hydrometer — should be ~1.089 (22.3°Bx). Target temperature: 65–70°F.
Additives — Day 0
Potassium metabisulfite (KMBS)required
¼ tsp per gallon (≈ 1.5g) dissolved in 50ml water — stir into must
Kills wild yeast and bacteria. Prevents oxidation at the most vulnerable moment. Essential first step. Wait 24h before pitching yeast.
⚠ Never add yeast within 12–24h of KMBS addition
Pectic enzymerequired
½ tsp per gallon — add simultaneously with KMBS or 1h after
Breaks down pectin from concentrate. This is the single most important step for white wine clarity and preventing dark yellow color. Without it, pectin stays in solution as a golden-brown haze.
Bentonite (pre-hydrated)optional — highly recommended
½ tsp per gallon — pre-hydrate in 50ml warm water for 30 min, then stir in
Early protein crash. Removes proteins that bind with phenolics to create golden-brown haze. Key fix if your Pinot Grigio came out too dark — add this at must prep, not just post-fermentation.
Sauvignon Blanc tip: Keep this variety under 68°F through fermentation for maximum aromatics (citrus, passionfruit). Add a cold crash at 38°F for 1 week before bottling for brilliant clarity.
Step 2 — Primary fermentation
Days 1–7: Active fermentation
Rehydrate and pitch yeast
Day 1 — 24h after KMBS
Dissolve Go-Ferm in 50ml water at 104°F. Add dry yeast, stir gently. Wait 20 min. Slowly add small amounts of must to cool the slurry to within 10°F of must temperature. Pitch into fermenter. Attach airlock.
Monitor fermentation
Days 2–7 · target 65–72°F
Airlock should bubble actively within 24–48h. Check gravity every 2 days. Add nutrients in 2 stages. Do not open fermenter unnecessarily — minimize O₂ for whites.
Additives — During primary
Go-Fermrequired (at pitch)
1.25g per gram of yeast — dissolve in 50ml water at 104°F, rehydrate yeast in it
Yeast rehydration protectant. Packs yeast cells with nutrients before they face the sugar-rich must. Healthier yeast = cleaner ferment, fewer off-flavors, zero H₂S risk.
⚠ Use in water only — never add Go-Ferm directly to the must
Fermaid-O (organic YAN)required
Split into 2 doses: 0.2g/gal at 24h after pitch · 0.2g/gal when SG reaches ~1.055
Provides nitrogen yeast need to reproduce and ferment cleanly. Staged addition is safer than one large dose. Prevents stuck fermentation and sulfur odors.
⚠ Do not add DAP after 1/3 sugar depletion (SG ~1.055) — produces fusel alcohols
Step 3 — Secondary fermentation
Days 7–21: Finish and clean up
Rack to secondary
Day 7–10 · when SG reaches 1.010–1.020
Siphon carefully off gross lees into a clean carboy. Minimize splashing — whites are highly oxygen-sensitive. Add KMBS (¼ tsp/gal dissolved in water). Attach airlock. Cool to 60–65°F.
Complete fermentation
Days 10–21 · FG target 0.992–0.998
Bubbling slows and stops. Confirm stable FG over 3 consecutive days. White wines do NOT undergo MLF — this preserves their fresh, citrusy acidity. Chardonnay exception: optional MLF for buttery style.
Step 4 — Clearing & aging
Weeks 3–8: Fining and stability
Kieselsol + Chitosan (dual-phase fining)required
Kieselsol: 1ml/gallon → wait 1h → Chitosan: 5ml/gallon
Best fining combo for white wines. Kieselsol (negatively charged silica) attracts proteins; Chitosan (positively charged) attracts yeast and tannins. Both fall as sediment together. Crystal-clear result in 1–2 weeks.
Isinglassoptional (if still hazy)
0.5–1g/gallon pre-dissolved in warm water
Fish-derived protein fining. Extremely gentle on flavor — ideal for delicate whites. Use only if kieselsol+chitosan alone isn't achieving the desired clarity.
Cold crashhighly recommended
Chill to 38–45°F for 5–7 days before bottling
Precipitates tartrate crystals and drops remaining particles out of suspension. The single best thing you can do for that Sauvignon Blanc-clear color you want.
Step 5 — Bottling
Weeks 8–12: Final preparation
KMBS final doserequired
¼ tsp per gallon dissolved in 50ml water — add and stir gently before bottling
Antioxidant protection in the bottle. Extends shelf life and preserves the fresh fruit character that makes whites so enjoyable. Without this, whites can turn golden-brown in the bottle within months.
Ascorbic acid (vitamin C)optional
¼ tsp per gallon at bottling — mix with KMBS
Secondary antioxidant. Works with KMBS to donate electrons and prevent oxidative browning. Especially helpful for Sauvignon Blanc and for wines that had any oxygen exposure.
Ready to drink: Chardonnay 3–6 months, improves to 12 months. Sauvignon Blanc best fresh — drink within 6–9 months for peak vibrancy. Store on side at 55–60°F.
Cabernet Sauvignon — CABs / CS World's most popular red · Bordeaux, France · Bold & age-worthy
The world's most popular red wine grape — loved for its high concentration, powerful tannins, and extraordinary age-worthiness. A natural hybrid of Cabernet Franc and Sauvignon Blanc, born in Bordeaux.
Origin & Type
The “wild Cabernet” from Bordeaux

Cabernet Sauvignon means “wild Cabernet” and originated in the Aquitaine Department of France, which includes Bordeaux. In 1997, researchers at UC Davis (Carole Meredith and John Bowers) shocked the wine world when they discovered that Cabernet Sauvignon was a natural hybrid child of Sauvignon Blanc and Cabernet Franc — two grapes that crossed naturally in the vineyards of Bordeaux centuries ago.

As Cabernet Sauvignon ages, it gradually loses its deep red color, shifting to a more garnet and eventually brownish-brick hue — a natural sign of a well-aged bottle.

Difficult to grow. Cabernet Sauvignon needs a sunny, warm (but not too hot) climate so the grapes ripen slowly. Too cold and the tannins turn harsh and green; too hot and it loses complexity. Gravelly, well-drained soils — like those in Bordeaux — are ideal.
Regions
Where Cabernet Sauvignon excels
🇫🇷 Bordeaux, France — the original
The “OG” Cabernet Sauvignon region. Gravelly soils produce some of the most savory and age-worthy expressions. Most wines here are blended into the famous “Bordeaux Blend” rather than made as single-varietals.
Flavors: Black Currant · Anise · Tobacco Leaf · Plum Sauce · Pencil Lead
🇺🇸 California, USA — Napa Valley, Sonoma, Santa Barbara
The North Coast AVA (Napa Valley, Sonoma) became world-famous when Californian wines beat France's top wines at the blind “Judgement of Paris” in 1976 — proving that great Cabernet could be made outside of France. Pacific Ocean breezes moderate temperatures along the coast.
Flavors: Black Currant · Blackberry · Pencil Lead · Tobacco · Mint
🇦🇺 South Australia — Coonawarra & Langhorne Creek
Warm climate and distinctive red clay “terra rossa” soils with high iron-oxide content. Famous for exceptional and unique expressions of Cabernet Sauvignon with ample depth, powerful tannins, and distinct notes of white pepper or bay leaf.
Flavors: Black Plum · White Pepper · Currant Candy · Chocolate · Bay Leaf
🇨🇱 Chile — Maipo, Aconcagua, Cachapoal & Colchagua Valleys
Chile offers some of the best value for exceptional quality Cabernet Sauvignon. Maipo Valley sits between cooling Pacific breezes and the hot Andes Mountains, creating one of the most ideal Mediterranean climates for the grape. Outstanding quality at accessible prices.
Flavors: Blackberry · Black Cherry · Fig Paste · Baking Spices · Green Peppercorn
🇩🇪 Pfalz, Germany & others
Germany's Pfalz region produces a smaller, cooler-climate style of Cabernet Sauvignon. Other notable regions include Washington State (USA), Tuscany (Italy), Mendoza (Argentina), and South Africa's Stellenbosch.
Flavors & aromas
Bold, dark, and complex — it all depends on the terroir

Some Cabernet Sauvignons are sumptuous and fruity, others are savory and smoky. It all depends on where the grape grows and how it is made. The unifying thread is its deep dark fruit character, firm tannin structure, and long finish.

Food pairing
Rich food for a rich wine

The rich flavor and high tannin content in Cabernet Sauvignon make it a perfect partner to rich, fatty, and boldly flavored foods. Tannins bind with proteins in meat, softening both the wine and the dish — a classic pairing principle.

🍄 Bold Dishes

Serving temperature: 60–65°F (15–18°C). Serve slightly below room temperature. Cabernet Sauvignon benefits from decanting for 30–60 minutes before serving to open up its aromas and soften the tannins — especially important for younger vintages.

Chardonnay AKA Morillon · Chablis (France) · Dry white
From lean and chalky to rich, creamy, and oak-aged — Chardonnay is the world's most versatile white grape, found in every winemaking country on earth.
Type & Body
Dry — grown everywhere, styled two ways

Chardonnay grows in a range of climates all over the world. It has two main style directions: oaked (rich, buttery, creamy) and unoaked (lean, mineral, crisp). It is also the primary grape in many of the world's finest sparkling wines, including Champagne, where it is blended with Pinot Noir.

Climate matters: Cool climates produce high acidity, light-to-medium body. Warm climates produce medium acidity, medium-to-full body — the same grape, entirely different wine.
Regions
Where Chardonnay shines

Chardonnay originated in Burgundy, France and is now found in every winemaking country in the world.

🌡 Warm climate — oaked style
France — Burgundy (Puligny-Montrachet, Meursault, Mâconnais)
Some of the world's finest Chardonnays. Oak aging, malolactic fermentation, and lees stirring create very complex, rich styles. Rich aromas of peach, mango, lemon, vanilla, butter, and toasted bread.
California — Napa Valley, Paso Robles, Lake County
Adapts to diverse climates. Tropical, opulent wines in warmer Napa Valley. Buttery, full-bodied style with vanilla and caramel from American oak.
Australia — Southern & Eastern regions, Mendoza (Argentina), Puglia (Italy)
Warm climate produces rich, fruit-forward Chardonnays with tropical notes and pronounced oak influence.
❄️ Cool climate — unoaked style
France — Chablis & Loire
Chablis uses Kimmeridgian clay soil for a unique flinty-mineral edge. Lean and steely with tart green apple, lemon, and oyster shell. Minimal oak. Yogurt aromas from MLF.
Sonoma Coast (CA), Oregon, Western Australia
Mineral-rich, cool-climate style. Refreshingly acidic with fruit-forward but restrained character.
New Zealand, Chile (Colchagua & Casablanca Valley), South Africa
Prominent cool-climate regions producing crisp, citrusy Chardonnays. Casablanca Valley in Chile is particularly notable for elegant, mineral-driven examples.
The two styles
Oaked vs Unoaked — completely different experiences
🪵 Oaked — Burgundian style
Aged in new oak barrels (American or French). Undergoes malolactic fermentation (MLF) + lees stirring.

Flavors: Vanilla · Butter · Caramel · Toasted bread · Peach · Mango · Tropical fruit

Serve at: 54°F (12°C)
🥶 Unoaked — Chablis style
Aged in stainless steel. No MLF. Preserves natural acidity and pure fruit expression.

Flavors: Green apple · Lemon · Pear · Citrus · Chalk · Mineral

Serve at: 48°F (9°C)
What is MLF? Malolactic Fermentation converts sharp malic acid into smooth lactic acid, raising pH and adding buttery/butterscotch aromas. It's nearly universal in red wines and common in oaked Chardonnay — but deliberately avoided in unoaked styles to preserve freshness. American oak adds sweet vanilla and coconut; French oak is subtler and more elegant.
Flavors & aromas
What to expect in the glass

Hints and strong fruity notes of pear, apple, tropical fruit, papaya, with hints of oak and vanilla in warmer expressions. Depending on ripeness: lemon and green apple (less ripe) to pineapple and figs (very ripe).

Sauvignon Blanc AKA Fumé Blanc (CA) · Bordeaux & Sancerre, France · Dry white
From the French sauvage (“wild”) — a highly aromatic, refreshing grape with racy acidity, green herbaceous notes, and vivid tropical fruit. One of the world's most recognizable white wines.
Type & Body
Mostly dry — with rare, prized sweet exceptions

Most Sauvignon Blanc is produced in a medium-bodied, dry style — refreshing and easy to drink, with moderate alcohol and elevated acidity. Sweet styles are rare but prized: the most famous are the dessert wines of Sauternes and Barsac in Bordeaux, made from grapes affected by botrytis cinerea (noble rot), which concentrates sugars and acidity by shrivelling the grapes on the vine, producing flavors of marmalade, saffron, and apricot.

Dry vs Sweet: Dry Sauvignon Blanc is fermented in stainless steel for crisp texture. Sweet Sauternes-style wines (Sauternes, Barsac) pair with desserts, pâté, or blue cheese — a completely different experience from the same grape.
Regions
Where Sauvignon Blanc thrives

Originates from Bordeaux, France — its name likely comes from the French words sauvage (“wild”) and blanc (“white”) due to its origins as an indigenous grape in South West France. It grows nearly everywhere and offers a variety of styles from lean to bountiful.

France — Loire Valley (Sancerre, Pouilly-Fumé)
Cool climate. The historic home of Sauvignon Blanc. Sancerre produces white wine of striking minerality, racy acidity, and citrus precision. Considered the world benchmark for the dry style.
France — Bordeaux (Sauternes, Barsac)
Blended with Sémillon for both dry whites and the famous sweet Sauternes dessert wines. Oak-aged dry Bordeaux Blanc has vanilla and custard notes with a riper tropical fruit profile.
New Zealand — Marlborough (South Island)
Cool climate, concentrated and complex. More citrus-focused than big and tropical — the pristine fruit shines with careful use of oak. Now the world's most recognized Sauvignon Blanc region outside France.
United States — Napa Valley, California & Washington
California's Napa Valley sometimes labels it Fumé Blanc when oak-aged. Washington produces vibrant, food-friendly examples with strong acidity and tropical character.
Chile — Andes Mountains & Casablanca Valley
The Andean altitude and Pacific influence produce crisp, herbaceous, and refreshing expressions at exceptional value.
South Africa, Australia, Austria & Others
South Africa (Cape Town coastal area), Australia (Adelaide Hills, Margaret River), Austria, Romania, Spain (Rueda), Argentina, Hungary all produce quality Sauvignon Blanc with regional character.
Maturation & winemaking
Stainless steel — the classic choice

Dry Sauvignon Blanc is almost always fermented in stainless steel tanks rather than oak, which preserves its crisp texture and vivid aromatics. Because it has so much character on its own, it is usually made as a varietal wine rather than blended with other grapes — a rare exception being Bordeaux Blanc (blended with Sémillon).

🥶 Unoaked (classic)
Stainless steel fermentation. Crisp, fresh, racy. Serve ice cold to highlight refreshing qualities. Lime · Grapefruit · Passion fruit · Grass · Green pepper
🪵 Oaked (Fumé Blanc)
Oak-aged style from Napa and Bordeaux. Serve slightly chilled. Vanilla · Custard · Riper tropical fruit · White peach · Toast
Cabernet Sauvignon connection: Cabernet Sauvignon is actually a cross between Sauvignon Blanc and Cabernet Franc — making Sauvignon Blanc a parent grape to one of the world's most famous reds. Other Bordeaux relatives include Merlot, Malbec, and Carménère.
Flavors & aromas
Vibrant, aromatic, unmistakably green

Sauvignon Blanc's herbal and vegetal aromas come from pyrazines — organic flavor compounds that create “green notes” in the wine: grass, green bell pepper, and jalapeño. These combine with vivid fruit to create one of the most recognizable aromatic profiles in wine.

Food pairing
When in doubt, go green

Sauvignon Blanc makes a wonderful choice with herb-driven sauces, fresh cheeses, and lighter proteins. The wine is light — keep your pairings light. It matches beautifully with Asian flavors like Thai and Vietnamese cuisine.

Serving temperature: Stainless steel style — serve ice cold to highlight refreshing qualities. Oak-aged (Fumé Blanc) — serve slightly chilled. Sweet Sauternes — pairs with desserts, pâté, or blue cheese.

Pinot Noir Light-bodied red · Burgundy, France · World's most popular light red
The world's most popular light-bodied red wine — and one of the oldest. Pinot Noir is a versatile, delicate grape that produces red, rosé, white, and sparkling wine. Hints of red fruit, flowers, and earth with a long, smooth finish.
Type & Body
Dry — light-bodied, surprisingly versatile

Pinot Noir is a dry wine by nature — with less than 3% residual sugar. While it may not seem as dry as a Cabernet Sauvignon or Tempranillo at first taste, it is firmly in the dry category. What makes Pinot Noir remarkable is that it is one of the few red grapes commonly made into red, rosé, white, and sparkling wine.

Did you know? DNA analysis has revealed that Pinot Noir, Pinot Gris, and Pinot Blanc are simply mutations of the same grape. The grape becomes progressively redder through mutation. Blanc de Noirs Champagne uses Pinot Noir (and Pinot Meunier) as its base grape — producing white sparkling wine from red grapes.
Regions
Where Pinot Noir grows best

Pinot Noir likely originated in Burgundy, France — one of the world's oldest wine regions, first tended by Cistercian monks in the Middle Ages. The vines prefer intermediate climates with long, cool growing seasons, often found in protected valleys or near large bodies of water. Pinot Noir enjoys the same climate as Chardonnay — the two grapes are often planted side by side.

🇫🇷 Burgundy, France — 75,760 acres (the benchmark)
The world's most coveted Pinot Noir grows on a narrow, east-facing slope just south of Dijon. Key villages: Nuits-St-Georges, Gevrey-Chambertin, Morey-St-Denis, Chambolle-Musigny, Vougeot, Vosne-Romanée, Aloxe-Corton.
Flavors: Cherry · Hibiscus · Rose Petal · Mushroom · Potting Soil
🇺🇸 California, USA — 73,600 acres
Sonoma, Sta. Rita Hills, Santa Lucia Highlands, Willamette Valley. Pinot Noir excels in places that receive cooling Pacific breezes and morning fog — Sonoma, Southern Napa, and the Central Coast (Santa Barbara). Typically rich, fruity, and lush.
Flavors: Cherry · Raspberry · Allspice · Darjeeling Tea · Vanilla
🇩🇪 Germany — 29,049 acres
Baden, Pfalz, Rheinhessen, Württemberg. Known locally as Spätburgunder. Elegant, earthy style with high acidity.
🇳🇿 New Zealand — 10,648 acres
Martinborough, Marlborough, Central Otago. Central Otago is the world's southernmost wine region and produces intensely flavored, structured Pinot Noir.
🌍 Other notable regions
Italy — Veneto, Alto Adige, Friuli (10,082 acres) · Australia — Victoria (8,693 acres) · Chile — Casablanca Valley, Limari, Maipú (7,127 acres) · Argentina — Río Negro (4,450 acres) · South Africa — Stellenbosch, Walker Bay (2,520 acres)
Flavors & aromas
Red fruit, earth, and elegance

Hints of red fruit (strawberry, cherry, cranberry), flower (hibiscus, rose petal), and spice aromas accentuated by a long, smooth finish. The earthy, forest-floor character is one of Pinot Noir's most distinctive signatures, especially in Burgundian examples. French oak barrels add subtle spice and structure without overwhelming the delicate fruit.

Food pairing
Versatile — higher acidity and lower tannin make it a great match

Pinot Noir's higher acidity and lower tannin make it one of the most food-friendly red wines. It pairs particularly well with duck, chicken, pork, mushrooms, and — unusually for a red — salmon.

Pinot Grigio AKA Morillon · Chablis (France) · Dry white
Fresh, zesty, and crisp white wine with pear, peach, and apricot notes. The names Pinot Gris and Pinot Grigio are technically interchangeable — Pinot Gris means gray Pinot in French, and Pinot Grigio is the Italian name for the same grape.
Regions
Where it grows
France — Burgundy (origin) & Alsace
Originated in Burgundy. The most famous sweet Pinot Gris comes from Alsace, France — richer, more unctuous with honey notes from botrytis.
Italy
Look for wines labeled Pinot Grigio from cooler climates: Alto Adige, Trentino, and Friuli-Venezia Giulia produce the crispest, most mineral expressions.
Germany — Grauburgunder
Commonly labeled as Grauburgunder. Look into the regions of Pfalz, Rheinhessen, and Rheingau for dry, structured examples.
Austria
Tiny plantings in Burgenland and Steiermark. Labeled as Pinot Gris or Grauburgunder.
Canada
Great potential from the Okanagan Valley (British Columbia) and Ontario. Fresh, fruit-forward style.
Others
Look into Chile, Hungary, Slovenia, and Romania for interesting expressions at great value.
Type & Style
Dry — but three distinct expressions

Pinot Grigio often tastes less sweet than Chardonnay because of its high acidity. There are three main types:

Minerally & Dry
Classic Italian style. Brilliant acidity, saline minerality, bitter almond finish. Alto Adige is the benchmark.
Fruity & Dry
New World style. More exaggerated fruit flavors — pear, peach, apple — with softer acidity.
Fruity & Sweet
The exception: either an inexpensive supermarket wine, or the rare slightly-sweet Alsatian Pinot Gris with botrytis honey notes.
Pinot Grigio vs Pinot Blanc: Blanc is made from a green grape. Gris is made from a light red grape — the juice is separated from the skins immediately to keep the wine pale. This is why Pinot Grigio typically originates from Italy and has that faint copper tint before clearing.
Flavors & Aromas
What to expect in the glass

Expect flavors of lemon, yellow apple, and white peach with a slightly richer, more oily texture. Primary fruit flavors: lime, lemon, pear, white nectarine, and apple. Depending on where the grapes are grown, Pinot Grigio can take on faint honeyed notes, floral aromas like honeysuckle, and a saline-like minerality.

Taste Profile by Origin
Acidity: Medium-high to high
Italian Pinot Grigio
Usually totally dry with brilliant acidity and a distinctive bitter almond finish. The leanest, most mineral style.
French Pinot Gris (Alsace)
Fleshy and more unctuous with faint honey notes from botrytis. Richer texture, lower acidity, longer finish.
American Pinot Grigio
More exaggerated fruit flavors and less acidity than European counterparts. Approachable and easy-drinking style.
Serving & Food Pairing
Serve ice-cold at 45°F (7°C)

With its zesty and refreshing acidity, Pinot Gris pairs beautifully with fresh vegetables, raw fish, and lighter meals. Fish and shellfish are the classic partners. Also great with ceviche, sushi, moules-frites, or light flaky tilapia with cream sauce.

Pinot Noir — dry red
Step 1 — Pre-fermentation
Day 0: Must preparation
Sanitize and prepare the must
Day 0
Sanitize all equipment. Rehydrate concentrate, top to 1 gallon. Measure OG (~1.089). Reds ferment slightly warmer than whites: target 68–75°F. This helps with color and tannin extraction.
Additives — Day 0
Potassium metabisulfite (KMBS)required
¼ tsp per gallon dissolved in water · wait 24h before yeast
Same as whites — essential protection at crush. Non-negotiable for all wines regardless of style.
Opti-Red (Booster Rouge)optional — highly recommended
½ tsp per gallon — add to must at Day 0
Grape-derived tannin and polysaccharide blend. Enhances color stability, mouthfeel, and body. Critical for 1-gallon concentrate batches where you can't do extended maceration with real grape skins.
Grape tannins (FT Rouge / Tannin Complex)optional
¼ tsp per gallon dissolved in small amount of warm water
Pinot Noir is naturally low in tannin — lowest of all reds. Adding grape tannins compensates for the lack of skin contact in concentrate wine, adding structure and aging potential.
Step 2 — Primary fermentation
Days 1–10: Active fermentation
Pitch yeast
Day 1 — 24h after KMBS
Best yeasts for Pinot Noir concentrate: Lalvin RC-212 (Burgundy character, spice, cherry) or Lalvin D254 (silky mouthfeel). Rehydrate in Go-Ferm at 104°F, cool to within 10°F of must, pitch. Ferment at 68–75°F.
Daily gentle stir
Days 2–7
Unlike whites, you can stir reds gently once daily (punchdown equivalent). This integrates any cap, distributes nutrients, and helps with color. Check gravity every 2 days. Target dry (SG 1.000) within 7–10 days.
Additives — During primary
Go-Fermrequired (at pitch)
1.25g per gram of yeast in 50ml water at 104°F
Healthy yeast start is even more important for reds — better color extraction, fewer off-flavors, clean finish.
Fermaid-O + DAPrequired
Fermaid-O: 0.3g/gal at 24h post-pitch · DAP supplement: 0.2g/gal at 1/3 depletion (SG ~1.055)
Staged nitrogen prevents H₂S and stuck fermentation. Fermaid-O provides organic nitrogen (preferred). DAP provides inorganic nitrogen top-up in the early stage only.
⚠ No DAP after SG ~1.055 — produces fusel alcohols
Step 3 — Secondary fermentation & MLF
Days 7–28: Dry out and soften
Rack to secondary
Day 7–10 · SG 1.010–1.020
Siphon off gross lees. Reds can handle a bit more splash than whites — a small amount of oxygen actually helps integrate tannins. Add light KMBS only (⅛ tsp/gal) — full dose would suppress MLF bacteria.
Malolactic culture (MLB)optional — strongly recommended
1 packet (sufficient for 1–6 gal) — inoculate when SG reaches 1.000–0.998 and temp is 65–72°F
Converts sharp malic acid → softer lactic acid. This is what gives Pinot Noir its signature silky, creamy, round mouthfeel. Takes 2–4 weeks. Confirm completion with paper chromatography strip or taste (gone tart edge = done).
⚠ Never add potassium sorbate if doing MLF — bacteria convert sorbate to geraniol, a permanent irreversible off-odor
Oak chips or spiralsoptional
Light French or American oak chips: ½ oz per gallon for 2–4 weeks — taste weekly
Adds vanilla, toast, cedar, and spice notes to Pinot Noir. Makes concentrate wine taste barrel-aged. French oak is subtler and more elegant — better match for Pinot's delicate character. Remove when desired flavor is reached.
Step 4 — Clearing & aging
Weeks 4–12: Patience pays off
Kieselsol + Chitosan (after MLF complete)required
Add full KMBS dose first (¼ tsp/gal). Then: Kieselsol 1ml/gal, wait 1h, Chitosan 5ml/gal
Clears reds with minimal stripping of color or tannin. Sediment falls within 1–2 weeks. Only add after MLF is confirmed complete — fining before MLF is done can strip the bacteria.
Rack every 4–6 weeks off lees during aging. Each racking improves clarity and integrates flavors. Check and refresh KMBS every 3 months — maintain 25–35 ppm free SO₂.
Step 5 — Bottling
Month 3–6: Into the bottle
KMBS final doserequired
¼ tsp per gallon dissolved in water — target 25–30 ppm free SO₂
Long-term protection in the bottle. Reds need slightly less sulfite than whites because tannins act as natural antioxidants. Still essential for preventing premature aging.
Drinking window: Drinkable at 6 months, noticeably better at 12 months, peak at 18–24 months for concentrate Pinot Noir. Store on its side at 55–60°F in a dark place.
Your Pinot Grigio diagnosis
Why white wines turn dark yellow — and how to fix it
Dark yellow or amber color in white wine comes from oxidation, pectin haze, protein browning, or warm fermentation. All four are preventable. Here's what likely happened and how to ensure your Chardonnay and Sauvignon Blanc stay crystal clear:
Cause 1: Oxidation during fermentation or racking
Exposure to oxygen oxidizes phenolic compounds in white juice, turning it amber-brown. Whites are far more sensitive than reds — reds have tannins that buffer oxidation; whites do not.
Fix: Keep whites under airlock at all times. No splashing during racking. Maintain sulfite at 25–35ppm. Add ascorbic acid at bottling as a second antioxidant layer.
Cause 2: Missing or insufficient pectic enzyme
Concentrates are high in pectin. Without pectic enzyme, pectin stays as a protein-polysaccharide complex that scatters light and gives wine a yellow-brown appearance even when technically clear.
Fix: Always add ½ tsp pectic enzyme per gallon at Day 0 — this is the #1 clarity intervention for concentrate whites.
Cause 3: Skipping early bentonite
Proteins in white juice bind with tannins and phenolics over time, creating golden-brown colloidal haze. Once this forms, it is very difficult to remove post-fermentation without over-fining.
Fix: Add ½ tsp pre-hydrated bentonite at Day 0–1. This early protein crash prevents the haze from forming in the first place.
Cause 4: Fermentation temperature too warm
Above 75°F, white wine ferments fast but runs "hot" — browning reactions accelerate, aromatics blow off, and the resulting wine looks and tastes flat and golden rather than bright and pale.
Fix: Ferment whites at 60–68°F. Slow, cool fermentation = lighter color, more vibrant aromatics, crisper finish.

Why Valles del Colibrí
Wine is more than a drink

Wine is more than an alcoholic beverage made from fermented grape juice. Chemically speaking, alcohol stimulates the release of neurotransmitters that produce pleasurable feelings — but wine is so much more than chemistry. It is an experience, sometimes social, that sharpens your sense of taste and deepens your ability to perceive quality, place, and craft.

Winemaking is both an art and a science. The ability to think outside the box, to improvise when needed, and to trust your senses — these are the skills that define a great winemaker. And above all: there is no good or bad wine. There is just wine. Your palate, your context, your moment.

"There is no good or bad wine — it is just wine."

Valles del Colibrí was created to transmit those experiences and that knowledge across the many dimensions of the wine world. Writing about wine means bringing that knowledge to readers so their encounter with wine is broader, deeper — an experience far beyond drinking. We write these notes to change the conception of wine, to give it a deeper meaning: knowing what to buy, making great decisions, and understanding the story in the glass.

Our home
Washington State — a world-class wine region

Washington is the second-largest premium wine producer in the United States, with over 1,000 wineries and 55,000+ acres of vineyards. The state's unique geography — dramatic temperature swings between day and night, volcanic soils, and abundant sunshine east of the Cascades — produces wines of extraordinary intensity and balance.

Chardonnay
Rich and complex, with apple, citrus, and creamy texture from Washington's warm days and cool nights.
Sauvignon Blanc
Bright and herbaceous with citrus zest and passionfruit. Washington's crisp nights preserve the vibrant acidity.
Pinot Grigio
Pale straw, mineral, and refreshing. Washington's cool growing season produces delicate aromatics and natural acidity.
Pinot Noir
Silky and elegant, with cherry, earth, and spice. Washington's volcanic soils give Pinot Noir a distinctive mineral edge.
Cabernet Sauvignon
Washington's signature red — bold, structured, with dark fruit, cassis, and firm tannins softened by the region's long, warm days and cool nights.
