Chardonnay & Sauvignon Blanc
Step 1 — Pre-fermentation
Day 0: Must preparation
Sanitize everything
Day 0, before anything else
Use potassium metabisulfite solution (1 tsp per gallon of water) to sanitize fermenter, airlock, spoon, hydrometer. Let air dry — no rinsing needed.
Prepare the must
Day 0
Add juice concentrate to fermenter, top with water to 1 gallon. Stir thoroughly. Measure OG with hydrometer — should be ~1.089 (22.3°Bx). Target temperature: 65–70°F.
Additives — Day 0
Potassium metabisulfite (KMBS)required
¼ tsp per gallon (≈ 1.5g) dissolved in 50ml water — stir into must
Kills wild yeast and bacteria. Prevents oxidation at the most vulnerable moment. Essential first step. Wait 24h before pitching yeast.
⚠ Never add yeast within 12–24h of KMBS addition
Pectic enzymerequired
½ tsp per gallon — add simultaneously with KMBS or 1h after
Breaks down pectin from concentrate. This is the single most important step for white wine clarity and preventing dark yellow color. Without it, pectin stays in solution as a golden-brown haze.
Bentonite (pre-hydrated)optional — highly recommended
½ tsp per gallon — pre-hydrate in 50ml warm water for 30 min, then stir in
Early protein crash. Removes proteins that bind with phenolics to create golden-brown haze. Key fix if your Pinot Grigio came out too dark — add this at must prep, not just post-fermentation.
Sauvignon Blanc tip: Keep this variety under 68°F through fermentation for maximum aromatics (citrus, passionfruit). Add a cold crash at 38°F for 1 week before bottling for brilliant clarity.
Step 2 — Primary fermentation
Days 1–7: Active fermentation
Rehydrate and pitch yeast
Day 1 — 24h after KMBS
Dissolve Go-Ferm in 50ml water at 104°F. Add dry yeast, stir gently. Wait 20 min. Slowly add small amounts of must to cool the slurry to within 10°F of must temperature. Pitch into fermenter. Attach airlock.
Monitor fermentation
Days 2–7 · target 65–72°F
Airlock should bubble actively within 24–48h. Check gravity every 2 days. Add nutrients in 2 stages. Do not open fermenter unnecessarily — minimize O₂ for whites.
Additives — During primary
Go-Fermrequired (at pitch)
1.25g per gram of yeast — dissolve in 50ml water at 104°F, rehydrate yeast in it
Yeast rehydration protectant. Packs yeast cells with nutrients before they face the sugar-rich must. Healthier yeast = cleaner ferment, fewer off-flavors, zero H₂S risk.
⚠ Use in water only — never add Go-Ferm directly to the must
Fermaid-O (organic YAN)required
Split into 2 doses: 0.2g/gal at 24h after pitch · 0.2g/gal when SG reaches ~1.055
Provides nitrogen yeast need to reproduce and ferment cleanly. Staged addition is safer than one large dose. Prevents stuck fermentation and sulfur odors.
⚠ Do not add DAP after 1/3 sugar depletion (SG ~1.055) — produces fusel alcohols
Step 3 — Secondary fermentation
Days 7–21: Finish and clean up
Rack to secondary
Day 7–10 · when SG reaches 1.010–1.020
Siphon carefully off gross lees into a clean carboy. Minimize splashing — whites are highly oxygen-sensitive. Add KMBS (¼ tsp/gal dissolved in water). Attach airlock. Cool to 60–65°F.
Complete fermentation
Days 10–21 · FG target 0.992–0.998
Bubbling slows and stops. Confirm stable FG over 3 consecutive days. White wines do NOT undergo MLF — this preserves their fresh, citrusy acidity. Chardonnay exception: optional MLF for buttery style.
Step 4 — Clearing & aging
Weeks 3–8: Fining and stability
Kieselsol + Chitosan (dual-phase fining)required
Kieselsol: 1ml/gallon → wait 1h → Chitosan: 5ml/gallon
Best fining combo for white wines. Kieselsol (negatively charged silica) attracts proteins; Chitosan (positively charged) attracts yeast and tannins. Both fall as sediment together. Crystal-clear result in 1–2 weeks.
Isinglassoptional (if still hazy)
0.5–1g/gallon pre-dissolved in warm water
Fish-derived protein fining. Extremely gentle on flavor — ideal for delicate whites. Use only if kieselsol+chitosan alone isn't achieving the desired clarity.
Cold crashhighly recommended
Chill to 38–45°F for 5–7 days before bottling
Precipitates tartrate crystals and drops remaining particles out of suspension. The single best thing you can do for that Sauvignon Blanc-clear color you want.
Step 5 — Bottling
Weeks 8–12: Final preparation
KMBS final doserequired
¼ tsp per gallon dissolved in 50ml water — add and stir gently before bottling
Antioxidant protection in the bottle. Extends shelf life and preserves the fresh fruit character that makes whites so enjoyable. Without this, whites can turn golden-brown in the bottle within months.
Ascorbic acid (vitamin C)optional
¼ tsp per gallon at bottling — mix with KMBS
Secondary antioxidant. Works with KMBS to donate electrons and prevent oxidative browning. Especially helpful for Sauvignon Blanc and for wines that had any oxygen exposure.
Ready to drink: Chardonnay 3–6 months, improves to 12 months. Sauvignon Blanc best fresh — drink within 6–9 months for peak vibrancy. Store on side at 55–60°F.
Pinot Noir — dry red
Step 1 — Pre-fermentation
Day 0: Must preparation
Sanitize and prepare the must
Day 0
Sanitize all equipment. Rehydrate concentrate, top to 1 gallon. Measure OG (~1.089). Reds ferment slightly warmer than whites: target 68–75°F. This helps with color and tannin extraction.
Additives — Day 0
Potassium metabisulfite (KMBS)required
¼ tsp per gallon dissolved in water · wait 24h before yeast
Same as whites — essential protection at crush. Non-negotiable for all wines regardless of style.
Opti-Red (Booster Rouge)optional — highly recommended
½ tsp per gallon — add to must at Day 0
Grape-derived tannin and polysaccharide blend. Enhances color stability, mouthfeel, and body. Critical for 1-gallon concentrate batches where you can't do extended maceration with real grape skins.
Grape tannins (FT Rouge / Tannin Complex)optional
¼ tsp per gallon dissolved in small amount of warm water
Pinot Noir is naturally low in tannin — lowest of all reds. Adding grape tannins compensates for the lack of skin contact in concentrate wine, adding structure and aging potential.
Step 2 — Primary fermentation
Days 1–10: Active fermentation
Pitch yeast
Day 1 — 24h after KMBS
Best yeasts for Pinot Noir concentrate: Lalvin RC-212 (Burgundy character, spice, cherry) or Lalvin D254 (silky mouthfeel). Rehydrate in Go-Ferm at 104°F, cool to within 10°F of must, pitch. Ferment at 68–75°F.
Daily gentle stir
Days 2–7
Unlike whites, you can stir reds gently once daily (punchdown equivalent). This integrates any cap, distributes nutrients, and helps with color. Check gravity every 2 days. Target dry (SG 1.000) within 7–10 days.
Additives — During primary
Go-Fermrequired (at pitch)
1.25g per gram of yeast in 50ml water at 104°F
Healthy yeast start is even more important for reds — better color extraction, fewer off-flavors, clean finish.
Fermaid-O + DAPrequired
Fermaid-O: 0.3g/gal at 24h post-pitch · DAP supplement: 0.2g/gal at 1/3 depletion (SG ~1.055)
Staged nitrogen prevents H₂S and stuck fermentation. Fermaid-O provides organic nitrogen (preferred). DAP provides inorganic nitrogen top-up in the early stage only.
⚠ No DAP after SG ~1.055 — produces fusel alcohols
Step 3 — Secondary fermentation & MLF
Days 7–28: Dry out and soften
Rack to secondary
Day 7–10 · SG 1.010–1.020
Siphon off gross lees. Reds can handle a bit more splash than whites — a small amount of oxygen actually helps integrate tannins. Add light KMBS only (⅛ tsp/gal) — full dose would suppress MLF bacteria.
Malolactic culture (MLB)optional — strongly recommended
1 packet (sufficient for 1–6 gal) — inoculate when SG reaches 1.000–0.998 and temp is 65–72°F
Converts sharp malic acid → softer lactic acid. This is what gives Pinot Noir its signature silky, creamy, round mouthfeel. Takes 2–4 weeks. Confirm completion with paper chromatography strip or taste (gone tart edge = done).
⚠ Never add potassium sorbate if doing MLF — bacteria convert sorbate to geraniol, a permanent irreversible off-odor
Oak chips or spiralsoptional
Light French or American oak chips: ½ oz per gallon for 2–4 weeks — taste weekly
Adds vanilla, toast, cedar, and spice notes to Pinot Noir. Makes concentrate wine taste barrel-aged. French oak is subtler and more elegant — better match for Pinot's delicate character. Remove when desired flavor is reached.
Step 4 — Clearing & aging
Weeks 4–12: Patience pays off
Kieselsol + Chitosan (after MLF complete)required
Add full KMBS dose first (¼ tsp/gal). Then: Kieselsol 1ml/gal, wait 1h, Chitosan 5ml/gal
Clears reds with minimal stripping of color or tannin. Sediment falls within 1–2 weeks. Only add after MLF is confirmed complete — fining before MLF is done can strip the bacteria.
Rack every 4–6 weeks off lees during aging. Each racking improves clarity and integrates flavors. Check and refresh KMBS every 3 months — maintain 25–35 ppm free SO₂.
Step 5 — Bottling
Month 3–6: Into the bottle
KMBS final doserequired
¼ tsp per gallon dissolved in water — target 25–30 ppm free SO₂
Long-term protection in the bottle. Reds need slightly less sulfite than whites because tannins act as natural antioxidants. Still essential for preventing premature aging.
Drinking window: Drinkable at 6 months, noticeably better at 12 months, peak at 18–24 months for concentrate Pinot Noir. Store on its side at 55–60°F in a dark place.
Your Pinot Grigio diagnosis
Why white wines turn dark yellow — and how to fix it
Dark yellow or amber color in white wine comes from oxidation, pectin haze, protein browning, or warm fermentation. All four are preventable. Here's what likely happened and how to ensure your Chardonnay and Sauvignon Blanc stay crystal clear:
Cause 1: Oxidation during fermentation or racking
Exposure to oxygen oxidizes phenolic compounds in white juice, turning it amber-brown. Whites are far more sensitive than reds — reds have tannins that buffer oxidation; whites do not.
Fix: Keep whites under airlock at all times. No splashing during racking. Maintain sulfite at 25–35ppm. Add ascorbic acid at bottling as a second antioxidant layer.
Cause 2: Missing or insufficient pectic enzyme
Concentrates are high in pectin. Without pectic enzyme, pectin stays as a protein-polysaccharide complex that scatters light and gives wine a yellow-brown appearance even when technically clear.
Fix: Always add ½ tsp pectic enzyme per gallon at Day 0 — this is the #1 clarity intervention for concentrate whites.
Cause 3: Skipping early bentonite
Proteins in white juice bind with tannins and phenolics over time, creating golden-brown colloidal haze. Once this forms, it is very difficult to remove post-fermentation without over-fining.
Fix: Add ½ tsp pre-hydrated bentonite at Day 0–1. This early protein crash prevents the haze from forming in the first place.
Cause 4: Fermentation temperature too warm
Above 75°F, white wine ferments fast but runs "hot" — browning reactions accelerate, aromatics blow off, and the resulting wine looks and tastes flat and golden rather than bright and pale.
Fix: Ferment whites at 60–68°F. Slow, cool fermentation = lighter color, more vibrant aromatics, crisper finish.
